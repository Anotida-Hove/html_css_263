What Is a Deepfake?

A deepfake is synthetic media — a video, image, or audio recording — in which a person's face, voice, or body has been replaced or manipulated using artificial intelligence. The technology was originally developed for film production and research, but free tools now make it accessible to anyone.

Deepfakes are built on a class of AI called generative adversarial networks (GANs), where two neural networks compete — one generates fake content, the other tries to detect it — until the fakes become indistinguishable from reality.

Real example: In 2024, a finance worker in Hong Kong transferred $25 million to fraudsters after attending a video call where every other participant — including his company's CFO — was a deepfake. He was the only real person on the call.

How Scammers Use Deepfakes

They find you online.

Scammers harvest photos and videos of your family members from Facebook, WhatsApp, and other social media. Even a few seconds of video or audio is enough to clone a voice.

They create a convincing fake.

Using cheap or free AI tools, they generate a voice clone or video of someone you trust — a grandchild, a parent, a coworker.

They manufacture an emergency.

A fake arrest. A fake accident. A kidnapping. The situation is designed to make you panic — because panic stops you from thinking clearly.

They demand immediate action.

"Don't tell anyone." "Send money now." "There's no time." These phrases are specifically designed to prevent verification.

How to Spot an AI Scam

Red flag: Any call or video creating sudden urgency, asking for money, and telling you not to call anyone else is almost certainly a scam — regardless of who appears to be calling.

Use a family code word.

Establish a secret phrase only your real family members know. If someone claims to be in danger, ask for the code word.

Hang up and call back.

Call on a number you already know. Don't use a number the caller gives you.

Look for visual glitches.

Look for unnatural blinking, lip sync issues, or strange lighting in video calls. Deepfakes often have subtle visual glitches.

Notice emotional pressure tactics.

Real emergencies don't usually require you to keep them secret.

Slow down.

Scammers rely on your panic. Even 60 seconds of pause can break their strategy.

Why Detection Is Difficult

Deepfake detection tools exist, but they are in a perpetual arms race with the generation tools. Every time a detector learns to spot one type of artifact, generators are updated to eliminate it. No detection tool is reliable enough on its own.

This is why awareness and human skepticism remain the most important defenses.

Ethics

The Ethical Issues of Deepfake Scams

These harms extend far beyond the individual victim — they affect identity, community, and trust itself.

  • Identity Theft

    Deepfakes allow anyone's face and likeness to be used without consent. A person's identity is tied to their dignity — having it weaponized to deceive the people they love is a violation that goes beyond financial loss.

  • Mental & Emotional Harm

    Victims frequently experience shame, anxiety, and lasting distrust of their own judgment. These scams deliberately trigger panic to bypass rational thinking — victims often blame themselves for something engineered to fool them.

  • Erosion of Trust

    When any video or image can be faked, communities grow suspicious. Families grow guarded. Shared digital spaces become environments of doubt, benefiting no one except those who profit from division.

  • Exploitation of Kindness

    These scams target the best parts of people — love for family, willingness to help, instinct to trust a familiar face. Every successful scam makes victims more guarded and less willing to respond to real emergencies.

  • Poverty as a Driver

    Many deepfake scam operations are run from compounds where workers — sometimes trafficking victims themselves — are forced to carry out fraud under threat of violence. Solutions that ignore poverty will have limited impact.

  • Diffuse Responsibility

    Responsibility spreads across scammers, software developers, app stores, social media platforms, and governments — each pointing to another. When accountability is this diffuse, no one is held responsible and victims have no recourse.

Ethics

Options for Action

Three different approaches — each with real trade-offs across ethical lenses.

Require AI Companies to Build Safety Into Their Tools

AI companies are made legally responsible for building detection tools, usage monitoring, and consent verification directly into their platforms before public release.

Pros

  • Addresses the problem at its origin
  • Reduces deepfake tool availability to scammers
  • Creates industry-wide standards

Cons

  • Small developers may not survive compliance costs
  • Scammers will seek unregulated overseas tools
  • Broad requirements may slow legitimate innovation

Ethical evaluation: Strong on the Utilitarian and Rights lenses. Weaker on Care Ethics since it doesn't directly support those already harmed.

Utilitarian ★★★ Rights ★★★ Justice ★★ Care Ethics ★

Government Regulation, Legal Penalties & International Cooperation

Governments pass and enforce laws criminalizing deepfake fraud, mandate watermarking of AI-generated content, and establish international agreements to pursue scam operations across borders.

Pros

  • Creates enforceable consequences
  • Gives victims legal recognition and recovery pathways
  • Watermarking makes deepfake content traceable

Cons

  • Legislation moves slowly vs. fast-evolving technology
  • International enforcement is extremely difficult
  • Overly broad laws risk suppressing legitimate AI uses

Ethical evaluation: Strongest on the Justice lens — the harms fall disproportionately on elderly people and low-income individuals, who deserve equal legal protection.

Justice ★★★ Rights ★★★ Utilitarian ★★ Communitarian ★★

Public Education, Bank-Led Warnings & Community Awareness

Governments, banks, and nonprofits invest in widespread public education campaigns. Banks take an active role by flagging suspicious transfers and offering scam literacy resources to clients.

Pros

  • Empowers vulnerable people immediately, without legislation delays
  • Banks have direct relationships with those most at risk
  • Reduces shame by framing scams as deliberate exploitation

Cons

  • Places burden on victims rather than perpetrators
  • Doesn't hold AI companies accountable
  • Uneven reach — most isolated people may never see campaigns

Ethical evaluation: Strongest on Care Ethics and the Communitarian lens. Community-level knowledge sharing strengthens the social fabric in ways laws and platform policies cannot alone.

Care Ethics ★★★ Communitarian ★★★ Utilitarian ★★ Justice ★
The verdict across ethical lenses: No single option produces the most good alone. A combination of all three — upstream regulation, legal enforcement, and community education — produces the greatest good for the greatest number of stakeholders.

Why This Matters

Generating deepfake content requires massive computing power. The data centers running it consume enormous amounts of electricity and water. As deepfake scams grow in scale, so does this environmental cost.

This burden falls disproportionately on communities near data center infrastructure — often lower-income or rural areas — who had no say in the matter and receive few of the benefits.

Environmental justice: The communities most affected by AI infrastructure are rarely the ones benefiting from the technology. This concern is almost entirely absent from mainstream conversations about AI scams — yet it deserves explicit recognition.

What Can Be Done

Requirements for renewable energy use and water recycling should be part of any serious policy response to AI regulation. Environmental advocates have an important role to play in pushing for sustainable AI infrastructure — connecting the dots between AI crime and ecological harm.

Discussion Questions

Think more deeply about the ethical dimensions of deepfake scams. Activate any question to expand it.

When a victim loses their savings to a deepfake scam, responsibility could be shared across the AI company that built the tool, the platform that distributed it, the bank that processed the transaction, and the government that failed to regulate it. In your opinion, who bears the greatest moral responsibility — and does spreading blame across so many parties make it easier for everyone to avoid being held accountable?
AI companies often argue their tools are general-purpose and not designed for harm. But deepfake scams were a predictable and well-documented misuse long before many of these tools were released. Should companies be legally and financially responsible for harms caused by foreseeable misuse of their products — even if they never intended that harm to happen?
Public education campaigns put some of the burden of protection on potential victims — people like elderly grandparents who never asked to be targeted by sophisticated AI technology. Is it fair to expect ordinary people to defend themselves against tools built by billion-dollar companies? Or does personal responsibility still play a role — and if so, how much?
Some people running deepfake scam operations are themselves victims of human trafficking, forced to work in scam compounds under threat of violence. Knowing this, how should the justice system treat them — as criminals, as victims, or both? And does addressing poverty and coercion need to be part of any meaningful solution to deepfake fraud?
If any video or image can be convincingly faked, how do families, communities, and institutions maintain trust in what they see? What replaces visual evidence as a way of knowing something is real — and what are the broader consequences for society when the most vulnerable people stop trusting their own eyes?

Help

Resources & What to Do

Practical steps if you or a loved one has been targeted — and how institutions can help.

I Got Scammed — Immediate Steps

  1. Do not feel ashamed. These scams are deliberately engineered by professionals to exploit emotion. Being targeted does not mean you were foolish.
  2. Contact your bank immediately and explain what happened. Ask about fraud reversal options for any transfers made.
  3. Report the scam to your national fraud reporting agency (in the US: FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov; FBI at ic3.gov).
  4. Document everything — screenshots, phone numbers, times, and amounts — before memories fade.
  5. Tell someone you trust. Shame keeps scams underreported and lets scammers keep operating.

My Loved One Was Scammed — How to Support Them

The most important thing you can do is not make them feel foolish. Deepfake scams are specifically engineered to bypass rational thinking. Anyone can be targeted.

What to say: "This happened because they designed it to fool people. It's not about how smart you are." Validation before problem-solving makes recovery much more effective.

Help them contact their bank and file a report together. Accompany them if they want to speak to authorities. Check in regularly — financial scams can have lasting psychological effects including anxiety and withdrawal.

Institutions & Banks

Banks are at a critical intersection — they have both the ability to detect fraud before it completes and a responsibility to protect clients' assets. Key actions institutions can take:

  1. Implement real-time warnings when clients receive calls from known scam numbers
  2. Flag suspicious wire transfer requests with a brief delay and human check-in call
  3. Provide scam literacy resources to all clients, especially elderly account holders
  4. Train staff to respond without judgment when clients report scam losses

FTC — Report Fraud

US Federal Trade Commission. File a report at reportfraud.ftc.gov. Reports help track patterns and lead to investigations.

FBI Internet Crime Center

For AI-assisted fraud and cybercrime, file a complaint at ic3.gov. Especially useful for large financial losses.

AARP Fraud Watch Network

Focused on protecting older adults from fraud. Helpline: 1-877-908-3360. Free resources and counseling.
